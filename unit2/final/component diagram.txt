### **UML Component Diagram**

#### **Definition:**
A **UML Component Diagram** is used to break down a large object-oriented system into smaller, manageable components. It provides a **physical view** of a system, showcasing elements such as **executables, libraries, files**, and how they interact within the system's structure.

---

### **Purpose of a Component Diagram:**
- To describe individual **components** of a system, not the functionalities.
- To visualize the **physical organization** of software components.
- To show how components like **libraries, packages, or files** are structured and connected.
- To represent the **static view** of the system at a given point in time.
- To aid in both **forward and reverse engineering**.
- To form a complete picture of the system when combined with other component diagrams.

---

### **Why Use a Component Diagram?**
- To illustrate the **functionality and behavior** of components in the system.
- To **visualize the system architecture** using interconnected components.
- To support **runtime analysis and system testing**.
- To show the **interaction between components and interfaces**.
- To enable better **system understanding and communication** among developers.

---

### **When to Use a Component Diagram:**
- When the system needs to be **divided** into multiple components based on functionality.
- To represent the **organization of components** within the system.
- During **system implementation** to visualize how components are integrated.

---

### **How to Draw a Component Diagram:**

**Step 1: Identify Artifacts**
- Determine what **files, libraries, and executables** are part of the system.
- Understand their **applications and relationships**.

**Step 2: Plan Layout**
- Use **meaningful names** for components.
- Create a **mental or rough layout** before formalizing.
- Add **notes** to clarify important details if needed.

**Step 3: Use UML Notations**
- **Component symbol**: A rectangle with two smaller rectangles (lollipops) attached on the side.
- **Interfaces**: Represented as **lollipops (provided)** or **sockets (required)**.
- **Dependency relationships**: Shown using **dashed arrows**.

---

### **Notation of a Component Diagram (Slide No. 12):**
- **Component**: Represented by a rectangle with the keyword `<<component>>` or a lollipop symbol.
- **Interfaces**: Use lollipop for **provided interfaces** and socket (half circle) for **required interfaces**.
- **Connectors**: Show how components are connected or dependent on each other using arrows.

---

### **Where to Use Component Diagrams:**
- To model **software components** of a system.
- To represent **database schemas**.
- To depict the **applications within an application**.
- To illustrate the **structure of source code**.

---

### **Key Characteristics:**
- Each **component is a replaceable and executable unit**.
- Components behave like **black boxes**, exposing only interfaces and hiding internal details.
- They facilitate the **development and testing** of complex systems by isolating functionality.
